Context

Description

Diabeatit is an educational health game aiming to teach children about diabetes. Made under UCSD Global Ties's Design For Development Lab for clients at the UCSD School of Medicine.

Responsibilities

  • Role: UX Team-lead, UX Designer, UI Designer
  • Team Size: 4
  • Tools: Figma, Pen and Paper, Google Docs
  • Skills: UX Design, UI Design, User Research, Usability Testing, Leadership
  • Time: Mar 2019 - June 2019

  • As team-lead, I was in charge of keeping track of the UX team's overall progress, leading meetings, and planning agendas, as well as listening to teammates and incorporating their voices into the project.
  • As a UX Designer, I used the UX process, methods such as wireframing, and usability testing to meet user and stakeholder needs.
  • As a UI Designer, I worked with my team on figuring out layouts and assisted with crafting a visual identity for the game's UI.

Problem

Due to a complete overhaul by the previous team, the UX team had to build off the previous team's work while introducing new elements for current and future needs. We also had to keep in communication with the Tech team (who were implementing the game in Unity) and Research (who were handling the knowledge behind diabetes).

Solutions

  • We created a mental model to help organize the game and direct game flow for players and future teams.
  • A UI was designed to help future teams better understand the game's creative direction and start a discussion on how to make the game look and feel fun for players.

Story / Game Structure

Navigation

The team first began organizing the logical flow of the game's navigation by grouping related functions and creating objects to place within the system.

My rough ideas of how the game should be organized

Plot Points

We plotted out the game's overarching narrative into plot points concurrently with its navigation. A typical 3-act story structure was fused with the navigation structure to ensure players would follow their avatar on their journey to learning about diabetes.

When converging we decided to combine our thoughts and ideas onto a white board drawing to ensure group comprehension.

Our finalized game structure/story flow.

UI: Wireframes

Lo-fi Sketches

Once we were all on the same page, we (along with someone from the Tech team) diverged to sketch lo-fi wireframes to come up with diverse ideas for presenting the navigation and story to players.

Upon convergence, this strategy proved useful when we combined UI elements from each member into a collective set of wireframes that better represented the UX team's intentions and what the Tech team were capable of.

Testing Paper Prototype

Time constraints caused us to do quick guerilla usabilty testing using three participants and specific-task based tests to elicit quick feedback for refining the UI's layout.

For example, a common issue was a lack of clear affordances with the buttons. Participants reported confusion with where they went or what their functions were. More can be seen in the User Testing Report.

UI: Digital Collaboration

Moodboard + Style Guide

Before we began redesigning the layout in Figma, we created some quick moodboards. The first displayed inspiration for how friendly childrens' game UI's should look.

The second moodboard was made to reflect the game's tone and mood regarding teaching kids about diabetes.

With the moodboards in mind, we made a styleguide to help ensure visual consistency across the game by defining the appearance of the UI elements and containers. Containers in this diagram are presented with their frames removed to help the team understand how layering worked with their contents.

Digital Prototypes

The UX team worked together in Figma because of its collaborative ability and perks from its free plan. I redesiged and implemented in Figma the Start Menu, Settings, Intro Dialogue, and Home screen.

User Testing Report

In the User Testing Report, we compiled our findings from usability testing in greater detail and posed questions for future teams to consider potential issues the UI may face as development continues and understand the rationale behind our decisions. I wrote everything except the "Results & Interpretation" section. Check it out!
